The product-brand fit analysis makes an assertion that elevates the portfolio from a collection of tools to a coherent system: "Not six unrelated tools. One operating philosophy, expressed as software."

Each product maps to a specific core value:

| Product | Function | Core Value |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| AutoProxy | Protects your attention | Leverage Over Labor |
| Stacker | Runs your operations | Right-Sized by Design |
| Playbook | Holds your knowledge | Self-Sufficiency as the Goal |
| Sentinel | Watches your business | Leverage Over Labor |
| Cashview | Clarifies your money | Radical Honesty |
| Dealflow | Tracks your deals | Builder's Mentality |

This mapping does strategic work on two levels:

**1. It creates coherence.** A portfolio that maps to values feels intentional, not opportunistic. When a customer asks "why these six products?" the answer is not "because we saw market opportunities" but "because these are the six things a soloscaler needs, each reflecting a principle we believe in."

**2. It constrains future development.** Any new product must map to a core value. If it does not, it either does not belong in the portfolio or reveals a missing value. This prevents scope creep and keeps the portfolio focused.

The Cashview-to-Radical Honesty mapping is the most interesting. A financial clarity tool mapped to honesty suggests the product will not flatter — it will show the real numbers, even when they are uncomfortable. This is a product personality derived from brand values, which is rare and powerful.
