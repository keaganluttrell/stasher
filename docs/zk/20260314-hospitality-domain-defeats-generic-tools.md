Hospitality internal messaging has five characteristics that generic AI tools handle poorly. Each creates a wedge that deepens AutoProxy's vertical advantage.

**1. Time-criticality varies wildly.** "Guest in 412 locked out" is urgent. "Can someone swap my Tuesday shift?" is not. Generic AI cannot distinguish without domain context -- it does not know that a lockout is an immediate safety and service issue while a shift swap can wait hours. This is why classification urgency must be domain-trained, not generic sentiment analysis.

**2. Role-based routing matters.** Housekeeping questions should not ping the front desk manager. Maintenance requests should not go to the concierge. Hotels have clear departmental boundaries that enable intelligent routing -- but only if the system understands the role taxonomy.

**3. Repeating patterns are extremely common.** "What time is checkout?" "Where are extra towels?" "Is the pool heated?" These operationally predictable queries have high-confidence answers, making auto-response defensible. The repeatability rate is much higher than in general office communication, which means the ROI of knowledge-base-driven auto-response is disproportionately high in hospitality.

**4. Shift-based workforce.** The "right person" changes every 8 hours. Static routing rules break. AI routing that understands schedules is materially better than any rule-based system. This also means the system must integrate with staff scheduling data, not just an org chart.

**5. Multilingual teams.** Hotel operations teams frequently span multiple languages. Translation-aware routing -- knowing that a Spanish-speaking housekeeper's question about "habitacion 412" is the same as the English checkout query -- is a genuine differentiator that horizontal tools do not prioritize.

Market timing reinforces this: Mews declared 2026 the "make-or-break year" for hotel AI, 78% of hospitality leaders plan to increase AI investment (Deloitte 2025), and first wins are expected in back-office operations and housekeeping.
