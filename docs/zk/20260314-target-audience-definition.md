Soloscaler defines its target audience with unusual precision, including an explicit "who this is NOT for" list. This negative definition is as strategically important as the positive one.

**Ideal clients:**
- Small business owners doing $200K-$5M in revenue
- Operators technical enough to appreciate smart systems but too busy to build them
- Founders who have outgrown DIY but refuse to overpay for bloated solutions
- People who want a partner, not a vendor

The revenue range ($200K-$5M) is a deliberate constraint. Below $200K, businesses typically cannot afford meaningful engagement. Above $5M, businesses start having the resources and complexity that justify enterprise solutions and dedicated teams.

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Businesses that want to outsource thinking (Soloscaler builds capability, not dependency)
- Anyone looking for the cheapest option ("cheap is expensive in the long run")
- Companies needing 200-person enterprise workflows ("go hire McKinsey")
- People who want a yes-man ("this brand has opinions")

The exclusions are doing heavy strategic work. Each one reinforces a positioning pillar: the outsourcing exclusion reinforces Independence Over Dependency; the cheapest-option exclusion signals premium positioning without saying "premium"; the enterprise exclusion keeps scope honest; the yes-man exclusion reinforces Radical Honesty.

The audience profile maps to a psychological archetype: the resourceful operator who respects competence, values directness, and measures success by capability gained rather than tasks completed.
