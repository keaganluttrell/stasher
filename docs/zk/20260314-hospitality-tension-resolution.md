The product-brand fit analysis addresses a strategic tension: Soloscaler's products (especially Stacker) have deep hospitality roots, but the brand positions as a general small business partner. The resolution is decisive: **lead with hospitality, never apologize for the vertical, expand deliberately.**

Four arguments support this:

**1. Vertical credibility converts to horizontal currency.** Proving your tools work in a demanding vertical is more persuasive than claiming they work for everyone. "Built for hospitality, works for any small business" is stronger than "built for everyone."

**2. Hospitality is a pressure cooker.** High volume, real-time operations, razor-thin margins, unpredictable demand, labor intensity. If tools work under these conditions, they work in less demanding environments. This is the same logic that makes military-grade a selling point in consumer products.

**3. Domain architecture enables expansion.** Stacker's composable domain design means adding new verticals does not require a rebrand or re-architecture. New domains stack on without disrupting existing ones.

**4. The Soloscaler brand never narrows to hospitality.** The umbrella brand stays general. Individual products can have vertical depth without limiting the brand. This is architecturally clean — the brand is the philosophy, the products are the implementations.

The validation pipeline echoes this: validate hospitality vertical first (narrower, more defensible), then test horizontal expansion. The order matters — proving depth before claiming breadth is more credible than claiming breadth and hoping depth follows.
