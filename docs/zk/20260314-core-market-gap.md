The Soloscaler brand identifies a three-sided market gap that leaves small businesses ($200K-$5M revenue) systematically underserved:

**Below: Freelancers** execute tasks but lack strategic thinking. You hire a Fiverr designer, you get a logo. You do not get a brand strategy. The business owner becomes the strategist, project manager, and integrator — the exact overhead they were trying to avoid.

**Above: Agencies and consultants** charge $10K-$50K/month retainers, build dependency rather than capability, and deliver through junior staff despite selling senior expertise. The economics do not work at small business scale. A $500K business cannot allocate $15K/month to an agency and survive.

**Sideways: Enterprise SaaS** tools are built for Fortune 500 workflows and adapted poorly for small business. They offer "Starter" plans that deliver 10% of the features for 80% of the complexity. This is the enterprise-lite paradigm — it treats small businesses as second-class citizens who should be grateful for a stripped-down version of someone else's product.

The critical insight is that small businesses do not need *less* of what big companies have. They need *different things*, built specifically for how they operate: lean, fast, resource-constrained, and wearing twelve hats at once. This reframing — from "small businesses need cheaper enterprise tools" to "small businesses need purpose-built solutions" — is the foundation of the entire Soloscaler product and brand strategy.

This gap is structural, not temporary. As long as the market builds for enterprises and adapts downward, the gap persists.
